**Nacre** ( [*NAY-kər*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"), [*NAK-rə*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")),[^1] also known as **mother-of-pearl**, is an organic–inorganic [composite material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_material "Composite material") produced by some [molluscs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mollusc "Mollusc") as an inner [shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seashell "Seashell") layer. It is also the material of which [pearls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl "Pearl") are composed. It is strong, resilient, and [iridescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridescence "Iridescence").

Nacre is found in some of the most ancient lineages of [bivalves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivalve "Bivalve"), [gastropods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastropod "Gastropod"), and [cephalopods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cephalopod "Cephalopod"). However, the inner layer in the great majority of [mollusc shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mollusc_shell "Mollusc shell") is [porcellaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porcelain "Porcelain"), not nacreous, and this usually results in a non-iridescent shine, or more rarely in non-nacreous iridescence such as *flame structure* as is found in [conch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conch "Conch") pearls.

The outer layer of cultured pearls and the inside layer of [pearl oyster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl_oyster "Pearl oyster") and [freshwater pearl mussel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freshwater_pearl_mussel "Freshwater pearl mussel") shells are made of nacre. Other mollusc families that have a nacreous inner shell layer include marine gastropods such as the [Haliotidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haliotidae "Haliotidae"), the [Trochidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trochidae "Trochidae") and the [Turbinidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbinidae "Turbinidae").

## Physical characteristics

### Structure and appearance

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Nacre_microscopic_structure.png/330px-Nacre_microscopic_structure.png)

Schematic of the microscopic structure of nacre layers

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Bruchfl%C3%A4che_eines_Perlmuttst%C3%BCcks.JPG/330px-Bruchfl%C3%A4che_eines_Perlmuttst%C3%BCcks.JPG)

Electron microscopy image of a fractured surface of nacre

Nacre is composed of hexagonal platelets, called tablets, of [aragonite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aragonite "Aragonite") (a form of [calcium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_carbonate "Calcium carbonate")) 10–20 [μm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%9Cm "Μm") wide and 0.5 μm thick arranged in continuous parallel [lamina](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lamina "wikt:lamina").[^2] Depending on the species, the shape of the tablets differs; in *[Pinna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinna_\(bivalve\) "Pinna (bivalve)")*, the tablets are rectangular, with symmetric sectors more or less soluble. Whatever the shape of the tablets, the smallest units they contain are irregular rounded granules.[^3] These layers are separated by sheets of organic matrix (interfaces) composed of [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_\(physics\) "Elasticity (physics)") [biopolymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biopolymers "Biopolymers") (such as [chitin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chitin "Chitin"), [lustrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lustrin_A "Lustrin A") and [silk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silk "Silk") -like [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein")).

Nacre appears [iridescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridescence "Iridescence") because the thickness of the aragonite platelets is close to the wavelength of visible [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light"). These structures [interfere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_\(wave_propagation\) "Interference (wave propagation)") constructively and destructively with different wavelengths of light at different viewing angles, creating [structural colours](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_coloration "Structural coloration").

The crystallographic *c-* axis points approximately perpendicular to the shell wall, but the direction of the other axes varies between groups. Adjacent tablets have been shown to have dramatically different c-axis orientation, generally randomly oriented within ~20° of vertical.[^4] [^5] In bivalves and cephalopods, the *b-* axis points in the direction of shell growth, whereas in the [monoplacophora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoplacophora "Monoplacophora") it is the *a* -axis that is inclined this way.[^6]

### Mechanical properties

This mixture of brittle platelets and the thin layers of elastic biopolymers makes the material strong and resilient, with a [Young's modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young%27s_modulus "Young's modulus") of 70 [GPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_\(unit\) "Pascal (unit)") and a yield stress of roughly 70 MPa (when dry).[^7] Strength and resilience are also likely to be due to adhesion by the "brickwork" arrangement of the platelets, which inhibits transverse crack propagation. This structure, spanning multiple length sizes, greatly increases its [toughness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toughness "Toughness"), making it almost as strong as [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon").[^8] The mineral–organic interface results in enhanced resilience and strength of the organic interlayers.[^9] [^10] [^11] The interlocking of bricks of nacre has large impact on both the deformation mechanism as well as its toughness.[^12] [Tensile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensile_testing "Tensile testing"), [shear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_stress "Shear stress"), and compression tests, [Weibull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weibull_distribution "Weibull distribution") analysis, [nanoindentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoindentation "Nanoindentation"), and other techniques have all been used to probe the mechanical properties of nacre.[^13] Theoretical and computational methods have also been developed to explain the experimental observations of nacre's mechanical behavior.[^14] [^15] Nacre is stronger under [compressive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compressive_stress "Compressive stress") loads than [tensile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensile "Tensile") ones when the force is applied parallel or perpendicular to the platelets.[^13] As an oriented structure, nacre is highly [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy") and as such, its mechanical properties are also dependent on the direction.

A variety of toughening mechanisms are responsible for nacre's mechanical behavior. The [adhesive force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesive_force "Adhesive force") needed to separate the proteinaceous and the aragonite phases is high, indicating that there are molecular interactions between the components.[^13] In [laminated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laminated "Laminated") structures with hard and soft layers, a model system that can be applied to understand nacre, the [fracture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fracture "Fracture") energy and fracture strength are both larger than those values characteristic of the hard material only.[^15] Specifically, this structure facilitates crack deflection, since it is easier for the crack to continue into the [viscoelastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscoelasticity "Viscoelasticity") and compliant organic matrix than going straight into another aragonite platelet.[^13] [^16] This results in the [ductile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ductility "Ductility") protein phase deforming such that the crack changes directions and avoids the [brittle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brittleness "Brittleness") ceramic phase.[^13] [^17] Based on experiments done on nacre-like [synthetic materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_synthesis "Chemical synthesis"), it is hypothesized that the compliant matrix needs to have a larger fracture energy than the [elastic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_energy "Elastic energy") at fracture of the hard phase.[^17] [Fiber pull-out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_pull-out "Fiber pull-out"), which occurs in other ceramic [composite materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_material "Composite material"), contributes to this phenomenon.[^16] Unlike in traditional synthetic composites, the aragonite in nacre forms bridges between individual tablets, so the structure is not only held together by the strong [adhesion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesion "Adhesion") of the ceramic phase to the organic one, but also by these connecting [nanoscale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoscale "Nanoscale") features.[^16] [^13] As plastic deformation starts, the [mineral bridges](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mineral_bridge&action=edit&redlink=1 "Mineral bridge (page does not exist)") may break, creating small asperities that roughen the aragonite-protein interface.[^13] The additional friction generated by the asperities helps the material withstand shear stresses.[^13] In nacre-like composites, the mineral bridges have also been shown to increase the [flexural strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flexural_strength "Flexural strength") of the material because they can transfer stress in the material.[^18] Developing synthetic composites that exhibit similar mechanical properties as nacre is of interest to scientists working on developing stronger materials. To achieve these effects, researchers take inspiration from nacre and use synthetic ceramics and polymers to mimic the " [brick-and-mortar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brickwork "Brickwork") " structure, mineral bridges, and other hierarchical features.

When dehydrated, nacre loses much of its strength and acts as a brittle material, like pure aragonite.[^13] The hardness of this material is also negatively impacted by dehydration.[^13] Water acts as a [plasticizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasticizer "Plasticizer") for the organic matrix, improving its toughness and reducing its shear modulus.[^13] Hydrating the protein layer also decreases its [Young's modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young%27s_modulus "Young's modulus"), which is expected to improve the fracture energy and strength of a composite with alternating hard and soft layers.[^15]

The statistical variation of the platelets has a negative effect on the mechanical performance (stiffness, strength, and energy absorption) because statistical variation precipitates localization of deformation.[^19] However, the negative effects of statistical variations can be offset by interfaces with large strain at failure accompanied by strain hardening.[^19] On the other hand, the [fracture toughness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fracture_toughness "Fracture toughness") of nacre increases with moderate statistical variations which creates tough regions where the crack gets pinned.[^20] But, higher statistical variations generates very weak regions which allows the crack to propagate without much resistance causing the fracture toughness to decrease.[^20] Studies have shown that this weak structural defects act as dissipative topological defects coupled by an elastic distortion.[^21]

### Formation

The process of how nacre is formed is not completely clear. It has been observed in *[Pinna nobilis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pinna_nobilis "Pinna nobilis")*, where it starts as tiny particles (~50–80 nm) grouping together inside a natural material. These particles line up in a way that resembles fibers, and they continue to multiply.[^22] When there are enough particles, they come together to form early stages of nacre. The growth of nacre is regulated by organic substances that determine how and when the nacre crystals start and develop.[^23]

Each crystal, which can be thought of as a "brick", is thought to rapidly grow to match the full height of the layer of nacre. They continue to grow until they meet the surrounding bricks.[^6] This produces the hexagonal close-packing characteristic of nacre.[^6] The growth of these bricks can be initiated in various ways such as from randomly scattered elements within the organic layer,[^24] well-defined arrangements of proteins,[^2] or they may expand from mineral bridges coming from the layer underneath.[^25] [^26]

What sets nacre apart from fibrous aragonite, a similarly formed but brittle mineral, is the speed at which it grows in a certain direction (roughly perpendicular to the shell). This growth is slow in nacre, but fast in fibrous aragonite.[^27]

A 2021 paper in *[Nature Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_Physics "Nature Physics")* examined nacre from *[Unio pictorum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unio_pictorum "Unio pictorum")*, noting that in each case the initial layers of nacre laid down by the organism contained spiral defects. Defects that spiralled in opposite directions created distortions in the material that drew them towards each other as the layers built up until they merged and cancelled each other out. Later layers of nacre were found to be uniform and ordered in structure.[^21] [^28]

### Function

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Fossil_nautiloid_shell_with_original_iridescent_nacre_in_fossiliferous_asphaltic_limestone.jpg/330px-Fossil_nautiloid_shell_with_original_iridescent_nacre_in_fossiliferous_asphaltic_limestone.jpg)

Fossil nautiloid shell with original iridescent nacre in fossiliferous asphaltic limestone, Oklahoma. Dated to the late Middle Pennsylvanian, which makes it by far the oldest deposit in the world with aragonitic nacreous shelly fossils. 29

Nacre is secreted by the [epithelial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epithelial "Epithelial") [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_\(biology\) "Cell (biology)") of the [mantle tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantle_\(mollusc\) "Mantle (mollusc)") of various molluscs. The nacre is continuously deposited onto the inner surface of the shell, the iridescent *nacreous layer*, commonly known as *mother-of-pearl*. The layers of nacre smooth the shell surface and help defend the soft tissues against [parasites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasite "Parasite") and damaging debris by entombing them in successive layers of nacre, forming either a blister [pearl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl "Pearl") attached to the interior of the shell, or a free pearl within the mantle tissues. The process is called *encystation* and it continues as long as the mollusc lives.

### In different mollusc groups

The form of nacre varies from group to group. In [bivalves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivalves "Bivalves"), the nacre layer is formed of single crystals in a [hexagonal close packing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagonal_close_packed "Hexagonal close packed"). In [gastropods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastropods "Gastropods"), crystals are [twinned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twinned_crystal "Twinned crystal"), and in [cephalopods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cephalopods "Cephalopods"), they are pseudohexagonal monocrystals, which are often twinned.[^6]

## Commercial sources

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Nacre_sticks.JPG/330px-Nacre_sticks.JPG)

Nacre bracelet

The main commercial sources of mother-of-pearl have been the [pearl oyster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearl_oyster "Pearl oyster"), [freshwater pearl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freshwater_pearl "Freshwater pearl") mussels, and to a lesser extent the [abalone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abalone "Abalone"), popular for their sturdiness and beauty in the latter half of the 19th century.

Widely used for pearl buttons especially during the 1900s, were the shells of the great green [turban snail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turban_snail "Turban snail") *[Turbo marmoratus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbo_marmoratus "Turbo marmoratus")* and the large top snail, *[Tectus niloticus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tectus_niloticus "Tectus niloticus")*. The international trade in mother-of-pearl is governed by the [Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species_of_Wild_Fauna_and_Flora "Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora"), an agreement signed by more than 170 countries.[^30]

## Uses

### Decorative

#### Architecture

Both black and white nacre are used for [architectural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architectural "Architectural") purposes. The natural nacre may be artificially tinted to almost any color. Nacre [tesserae](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tessera "wikt:tessera") may be cut into shapes and [laminated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamination "Lamination") to a [ceramic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceramic "Ceramic") [tile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tile "Tile") or [marble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marble "Marble") base. The tesserae are hand-placed and closely sandwiched together, creating an irregular mosaic or pattern (such as a weave). The laminated material is typically about 2 millimetres (0.079 in) thick. The tesserae are then [lacquered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lacquer "Lacquer") and [polished](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polishing "Polishing") creating a durable and glossy surface. Instead of using a marble or tile base, the nacre tesserae can be glued to [fiberglass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiberglass "Fiberglass"). The result is a lightweight material that offers a seamless installation and there is no limit to the sheet size. Nacre sheets may be used on interior floors, exterior and interior walls, countertops, doors and ceilings. Insertion into architectural elements, such as columns or furniture is easily accomplished.

#### Jewelry

Mother of pearl is commonly used in jewelry due to its smooth texture and iridescent appearance. It is sourced from the inner layer of mollusk shells, such as oysters and [abalones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abalone "Abalone").

Mother of pearl is frequently crafted into earrings, pendants, rings, bracelets, and brooches. It can be carved into various shapes or inlaid into metal settings, often combined with gold, silver, or gemstones. The material is valued for its natural luster and the subtle color variations it displays, which can include white, cream, pink, and green.[^31]

#### Musical instruments

Nacre inlay is often used for music [keys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Key_\(instrument\) "Key (instrument)") and other decorative motifs on musical instruments. Many [accordion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accordion "Accordion") and [concertina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concertina "Concertina") bodies are completely covered in nacre, and some [guitars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guitar "Guitar") have fingerboard or headstock inlays made of nacre (or imitation [pearloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearloid "Pearloid") plastic inlays). The [bouzouki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouzouki "Bouzouki") and [baglamas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baglamas "Baglamas") (Greek plucked string instruments of the [lute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lute "Lute") family) typically feature nacre decorations, as does the related Middle Eastern [oud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oud "Oud") (typically around the [sound holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_hole "Sound hole") and on the back of the instrument). [Bows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_\(music\) "Bow (music)") of stringed instruments such as the [violin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Violin "Violin") and [cello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cello "Cello") often have mother-of-pearl inlay at the frog. It is traditionally used on [saxophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saxophone "Saxophone") keytouches, as well as the valve buttons of [trumpets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trumpets "Trumpets") and other brass instruments. The Middle Eastern [goblet drum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goblet_drum "Goblet drum") (darbuka) is commonly decorated by mother-of-pearl.

#### Indian mother-of-pearl art

At the end of 19th century, Anukul Munsi was the first artist who successfully carved the shells of [oysters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oyster "Oyster") into the shape of a human being. For the [British Empire Exhibition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire_Exhibition "British Empire Exhibition") in 1924, he received a gold medal.[^32] [^33] His eldest son [Annada Munshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annada_Munsi "Annada Munsi") is credited with drawing *Indian Swadesi Movement* in the form of Indian advertising.[^34] Anukul Charan Munshi's third son [Manu Munshi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manu_Munsi "Manu Munsi") was felt to be one of the finest mother-of-pearl artists in the middle of the 20th century. As the best example of "Charu and Karu art of Bengal," the former [Chief Minister of West Bengal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chief_Minister_of_West_Bengal "Chief Minister of West Bengal"), Dr. [Bidhan Chandra Roy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bidhan_Chandra_Roy "Bidhan Chandra Roy"), sent Manu's artwork, "Gandhiji's Noakhali Abhiyan", to the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"). Numerous figures, such as [Satyajit Ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyajit_Ray "Satyajit Ray"), [Bidhan Chandra Roy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bidhan_Chandra_Roy "Bidhan Chandra Roy"), Barrister Subodh Chandra Roy, [Subho Tagore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subho_Tagore "Subho Tagore"), [Humayun Kabir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humayun_Kabir_\(Bengal_politician\) "Humayun Kabir (Bengal politician)"), [Jehangir Kabir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jehangir_Kabir "Jehangir Kabir"), as well as his elder brother Annada Munshi, were among the patrons of his works of art. " [Indira Gandhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indira_Gandhi "Indira Gandhi") " was one of his famous mother of pearl works of art. He is credited with portraying Tagore in various creative stances that were skillfully carved into metallic plates.[^35] [^36] His cousin Pratip Munshi was also a mother-of-pearl artist.[^37] [^38]

#### Other

Mother-of-pearl [buttons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Button "Button") are used in clothing either for functional or decorative purposes. The [Pearly Kings and Queens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearly_Kings_and_Queens "Pearly Kings and Queens") are an elaborate example of this.

Mother-of-pearl is sometimes used to make [spoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spoon "Spoon") -like utensils for [caviar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caviar "Caviar") (i.e. caviar servers [^39] [^40]) so as to not spoil the taste with metallic spoons.

## Manufactured nacre

In 2012, researchers created calcium-based nacre in the laboratory by mimicking its natural growth process.[^41]

In 2014, researchers used lasers to create an analogue of nacre by engraving networks of wavy 3D "micro-cracks" in glass. When the slides were subjected to an impact, the micro-cracks absorbed and dispersed the energy, keeping the glass from shattering. Altogether, treated glass was reportedly 200 times tougher than untreated glass.[^42]
