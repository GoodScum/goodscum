a poem by an old friend

"Royal decree: no leaving me," she said

into the grass, to the silver beetles,

small and shuddering on the yellow leaves

like specks of graphite. She folded herself

under a hill of green tulle and pulled dark

twigs from the waves in her

hair to pile over her

ribs. She had to stay

still, but I touched her shoulder

and she shifted, and the twigs

fell. I remember peeling apart

flat helicopter leaves for the sap.

I peeled the button out of the fabric

behind her neck and my lips

were dry, coarse as I carved just her

name into the bark of a young tree.

### moldavite — 2016-07 `#10871930`

A fish scoots around it’s tank,

forgetting each second as it passes.

It turns a small loop underneath

a plastic leaf, and slides itself

through the artificially blue water

to look at me, right in the eyes.

She floats there for one moment,

and forgets me in the next.

Scooping a pebble into her mouth,

she carries it over a few inches

before spitting it out, no place

in particular. I imagine that she wondered, 

“Why is there a pebble in my mouth?”

And in some strange, stupid, 

violently-human way, I resent her.

I resent this fish, and it’s tiny,

boring, zen-by-default life.

In a way that grips my heart, and 

makes me question my intelligence,

I wish and wish and wish

that I could breathe water.
