I was 18 years old and full of magic. Brimming with it in the way that only the young can brim and never deplete. It was a beautiful existence that could only happen to the person that I was then. Freedom from responsibility or hardship; cradled by an unbelievable kindness, in retrospect.

I lived near Austin, TX with a couple in their mid-20’s. I was in love with them both, in that way that only the young can - without depletion.  They loved me too, in their careful way, and let me stay with them rent-free for quite a long time, while I waited between seasonal jobs in Yellowstone.

I spent almost all of my time reading books about philosophy, watching documentaries about quantum physics, meditating frequently, learning to lucid dream. Every day I would walk two miles to the library and study whatever I felt the need to learn.

The city I lived in was largely concrete and utilitarian, apart from this one square block of unused and overgrown land that I’d found one day while aimlessly exploring. It was meager, littered with abandoned city debris and remnants of the drunk and homeless. A concrete water canal flowed weakly through it’s center.

I fell in love with that small forest; I went there every day. I’d sit on the littered bank of the murky water and swat at mosquitoes for hours. I made friends with every kind of bug and beetle. I named the trees. In that space I sang, I danced, I cried.

Eventually, in my effort to know every inch of the forest by heart, I found a beehive. The small golden maidens had built their home inside of an abandoned semi-truck tire. At a very specific time of day, the sun beamed directly at it through the leaves; practically bleeding amber propolis and honey, the hive was very obviously the center of the the forest. The bees were calm to the extent that I sat inches from the hive everyday, admiring, and never once received a sting.

In finding it’s heart, I named that oasis “The Kingdom.”

On December 23rd - 2011, my personal holiday, I ate some sacred mushrooms and trekked through the heat to, at that time, my favorite place in the world. On the way to the hive, I collected empty snail shells and left trails of tangerine peels in my wake. Thorns bit me as I scrambled through the brush, and I laughed and laughed.

As I approached the hive, something seemed…off. Like someone had been tampering with my Kingdom. It was a vague feeling, ignored for it’s oddity, until I got close enough to learn the cause of the discomfort in my gut.

Someone had burned the beehive to cinders. Nothing was left of it.

A pile of ashes where once an entire family of gold-bearing creatures once lived. I wept, and wept, as if for my own family. I couldn’t understand how such a thing could happen, nor fathom the damaged soul that would commit such an atrocity. It was senseless. Egregious.

I wept, and once I’d poured my dense sorrow into the earth, I listened and listened closely. Silent anguish filled the air, until a voiceless request blew through my spirit:

“Be My Heart.”

No consideration necessary, intent clear as crystal, I replied a voiceless, “Yes.”

With every particle I accepted, and for the months before my return to the mountains of Montana, I acted as the pumping heart of that small, beautiful little paradise. A few deer (rare, in this city) slowly made their way across the glade and drank.

It was the least I could do.
